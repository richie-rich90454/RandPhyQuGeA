## 1. Course Fundamentals

### 1.1 Course Identity
- **Name:** Physics 1: Algebra‑Based
- **Level:** Introductory college‑level physics
- **Mathematical prerequisite:** Geometry completed; concurrent Algebra II or equivalent (trigonometry included in course)
- **Laboratory requirement:** **25% of instructional time** hands‑on, inquiry‑based investigations
- **College equivalent:** First semester of algebra‑based introductory physics

### 1.2 Core Principles (adapted from original page 3)
- The course values clarity, transparency, evidence‑based reasoning, and intellectual freedom.
- Censorship of required topics (e.g., evolution in biology) is not permitted.
- No indoctrination: students analyze different perspectives, never required to feel certain ways.
- Open‑minded study of different cultures and histories is essential.
- Every student is listened to and respected; respectful debate is cultivated.
- Parents and students freely choose to enroll; course materials are crafted by committees of professors and expert educators.

### 1.3 Course Content Areas (Eight Units)
1. Kinematics
2. Force and Translational Dynamics
3. Work, Energy, and Power
4. Linear Momentum
5. Torque and Rotational Dynamics

## 2. Science Practices (Skills) – The “How”

Three broad practices, each with specific skills. Every final exam question aligns a learning objective (content) with a skill.

### Practice 1: Creating Representations

| Skill | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **1.A** | Create diagrams, tables, charts, schematics to represent physical situations |
| **1.B** | Create quantitative graphs with appropriate scales and units, including plotting data |
| **1.C** | Create qualitative sketches of graphs representing features of a model or behavior of a system |

### Practice 2: Mathematical Routines

| Skill | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **2.A** | Derive a symbolic expression from known quantities by selecting and following a logical mathematical pathway |
| **2.B** | Calculate or estimate an unknown quantity with units from known quantities |
| **2.C** | Compare physical quantities between two or more scenarios or at different times/locations in a single scenario |
| **2.D** | Predict new values or factors of change using functional dependence between variables |

### Practice 3: Scientific Questioning and Argumentation

| Skill | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **3.A** | Create experimental procedures appropriate for a given scientific question |
| **3.B** | Apply an appropriate law, definition, theoretical relationship, or model to make a claim |
| **3.C** | Justify or support a claim using evidence from experimental data, physical representations, or physical principles/laws |

### UNIT 1 – KINEMATICS (10–15%, ~12–17 periods)

**Essential Questions:**
- How can frames of reference allow two people to tell the truth yet have conflicting reports?
- How to estimate building height with a rock and stopwatch?
- Why might you seem to move backwards when a car passes?
- Why “double speed → four times stopping distance”?

**Developing Understanding:** Foundation for all motion; acceleration; multiple representations.

**Building Science Practices:** 1.A, 1.C, 2.A, 2.B – graphical and mathematical forms, frames of reference.

**Preparing for Final Assessment:** Emphasis on Translation Between Representations (TBR) questions – sketches, graphs, equations, verbal descriptions.

#### Topic 1.1: Scalars and Vectors in One Dimension

**LO 1.1.A** – Describe a scalar/vector using magnitude and direction.  
**EK 1.1.A.1** – Scalars: magnitude only; vectors: magnitude and direction.  
**EK 1.1.A.2** – Vectors as arrows (direction, length proportional to magnitude).  
**EK 1.1.A.3** – Distance, speed = scalars; position, displacement, velocity, acceleration = vectors.  
- Vector notation: arrow above symbol.  
- Relevant equation: \(\vec{v} = \vec{v}_0 + \vec{a}t\)  
- Derived equation (1‑D): \(v_x = v_{x0} + a_x t\)  
- In 1‑D, sign of component gives direction.  
**LO 1.1.B** – Describe vector sum in one dimension.  
**EK 1.1.B.1** – Opposite directions denoted by opposite signs.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.A, 2.C, 3.B, 3.C

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
A car moves 5 m east, then 3 m west. What is the magnitude of its displacement?  
(A) 2 m (B) 5 m (C) 8 m (D) 3 m → **Answer: A**

**Sample Word Problem (Free‑Response):**  
A jogger runs 200 m north, then 150 m south.  
a) Calculate the total distance traveled.  
b) Calculate the displacement (magnitude and direction).  
c) If the jogger takes 100 seconds, what is the average speed? Average velocity?  
*(Answers: a) 350 m; b) 50 m north; c) avg speed = 3.5 m/s, avg velocity = 0.5 m/s north)*

---

#### Topic 1.2: Displacement, Velocity, and Acceleration

**LO 1.2.A** – Describe change in position.  
**EK 1.2.A.1** – Object model: ignore size/shape; treat as point with mass, charge.  
**EK 1.2.A.2** – Displacement \(\Delta x = x - x_0\).  
**LO 1.2.B** – Describe average velocity and acceleration.  
**EK 1.2.B.1** – Averages use initial and final states over interval.  
**EK 1.2.B.2** – \(\vec{v}_{\text{avg}} = \Delta\vec{x}/\Delta t\)  
**EK 1.2.B.3** – \(\vec{a}_{\text{avg}} = \Delta\vec{v}/\Delta t\)  
**LO 1.2.C** – Describe instantaneous velocity and acceleration.  
**EK 1.2.C.4** – Accelerating if magnitude and/or direction of velocity changes.  
**EK 1.2.C.5** – Over very small Δt, average ≈ instantaneous.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.C, 2.B, 2.C, 3.C

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
A particle moves from x = 2 m to x = 8 m in 3 seconds, then back to x = 5 m in 2 seconds. What is the magnitude of its average velocity over the whole 5 seconds?  
(A) 0.6 m/s (B) 1.2 m/s (C) 2.4 m/s (D) 3.0 m/s → **Answer: A** (displacement = 3 m, time = 5 s)

**Sample Word Problem:**  
A ball is thrown straight up. Its height as a function of time is given by \(h(t) = 20t - 5t^2\) (h in meters, t in seconds).  
a) Find the average velocity from t=0 to t=2 s.  
b) Find the instantaneous velocity at t=1 s.  
c) When does the ball reach its maximum height?  
*(Answers: a) 10 m/s; b) 10 m/s; c) t=2 s)*

#### Topic 1.3: Representing Motion

**LO 1.3.A** – Describe motion using representations.  
**EK 1.3.A.1** – Representations: motion diagrams, figures, graphs, equations, narratives.  
**EK 1.3.A.2** – Constant acceleration kinematic equations (1‑D):  
\(v_x = v_{x0} + a_x t\)  
\(x = x_0 + v_{x0}t + \frac12 a_x t^2\)  
\(v_x^2 = v_{x0}^2 + 2a_x(x - x_0)\)  
**EK 1.3.A.3** – Near Earth surface, \(g \approx 10\ \text{m/s}^2\) (9.8 allowed without penalty).  
**EK 1.3.A.4** – Graphs:  
- Instantaneous acceleration = slope of tangent on v‑t graph.  
- Displacement = area under v‑t curve.  
- Change in velocity = area under a‑t curve.  
**Boundary:** Nonuniform acceleration – qualitative only, no quantitative analysis.  
**Boundary (g):** Use 10 m/s² unless specified; 9.8 or 9.81 acceptable.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.C, 2.A, 2.C, 3.B

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
The graph of velocity vs. time for an object is a straight line with positive slope. Which statement is true?  
(A) Acceleration is constant and positive. (B) Acceleration is increasing. (C) Displacement is zero. (D) The object is at rest. → **Answer: A**

**Sample Free‑Response / Word Problem:**  
A car accelerates from rest at 2 m/s² for 5 seconds, then travels at constant speed for 10 seconds, then brakes to a stop with constant deceleration of 4 m/s².  
a) Sketch the v‑t graph.  
b) Calculate the total distance traveled.  
c) Calculate the average speed.  
*(Answers: b) 25 m + 100 m + 12.5 m = 137.5 m; c) 137.5 m / (5+10+2.5)=137.5/17.5≈7.86 m/s)*

**Additional Word Problem (Graphing):**  
Given the following position‑time data: t(s): 0,1,2,3,4; x(m): 0, 2, 8, 18, 32.  
a) Plot x vs t.  
b) Determine the shape – what does it suggest about acceleration?  
c) Calculate instantaneous velocity at t=2 s using the slope of a tangent.

#### Topic 1.4: Reference Frames and Relative Motion

**LO 1.4.A** – Describe reference frame of an observer.  
**EK 1.4.A.1** – Choice of frame determines direction/magnitude of quantities.  
**LO 1.4.B** – Describe motion as measured in different inertial frames.  
**EK 1.4.B.1** – Measurements can be converted between frames.  
**EK 1.4.B.2** – Observed velocity = combination of object’s velocity + observer’s frame velocity.  
- Vector addition/subtraction.  
- Acceleration same in all inertial frames.  
**Boundary:** Frames assumed inertial unless stated. Relative velocities restricted to one dimension.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.C, 2.A, 2.B, 3.C

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
A train moves at 20 m/s east. A passenger walks toward the front of the train at 2 m/s relative to the train. What is the passenger’s velocity relative to the ground?  
(A) 18 m/s east (B) 18 m/s west (C) 22 m/s east (D) 22 m/s west → **Answer: C**

**Sample Word Problem:**  
A boat can travel at 8 m/s in still water. It aims straight across a river that flows at 3 m/s. The river is 40 m wide.  
a) What is the boat’s velocity relative to the ground (magnitude and direction)?  
b) How long does it take to cross?  
c) How far downstream does it land?  
*(Answers: a) √(8²+3²)=√73≈8.54 m/s, angle tan⁻¹(3/8)=20.6° downstream; b) 40/8=5 s; c) 3*5=15 m)*

#### Topic 1.5: Vectors and Motion in Two Dimensions

**LO 1.5.A** – Describe perpendicular components of a vector.  
**EK 1.5.A.1** – Vector as resultant of two perpendicular components.  
**EK 1.5.A.2** – Resolve using chosen coordinate system.  
**EK 1.5.A.3** – Use trig: \(\sin\theta = a/c\), \(\cos\theta = b/c\), \(\tan\theta = a/b\), \(a^2+b^2=c^2\)  
**LO 1.5.B** – Describe motion in two dimensions.  
**EK 1.5.B.1** – Analyze 2‑D motion by separating into 1‑D components.  
**EK 1.5.B.2** – Projectile motion: zero acceleration in one dimension, constant nonzero acceleration in the other.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.B, 2.A, 2.D, 3.A, 3.C

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
A ball is launched at 20 m/s at 30° above horizontal. What is its initial vertical velocity component?  
(A) 10 m/s (B) 10√3 m/s (C) 20 m/s (D) 0 m/s → **Answer: A** (20 sin30° = 10)

**Sample Word Problem (Projectile Motion):**  
A projectile is fired from ground level with speed 30 m/s at 40° above horizontal.  
a) Find the time of flight.  
b) Find the range.  
c) Find the maximum height.  
*(Use g=10 m/s²) Answers: a) 2*30 sin40°/10 ≈ 3.86 s; b) 30 cos40° * 3.86 ≈ 88.7 m; c) (30 sin40°)²/(2*10) ≈ 18.6 m*

**Extended Free‑Response (Experimental Design):**  
Design an experiment to determine the launch speed of a spring‑loaded projectile launcher using only a meter stick and a stopwatch. Describe your procedure, the quantities you will measure, how you will reduce uncertainty, and how you will calculate the speed.

**Building Science Practices:** 2.A, 2.D, 3.B, 3.C – derivations, predictions, claims.

**Preparing for Final Assessment:** Qualitative/Quantitative Translation (QQT) questions – claim without equations, then derive, then connect.

#### Topic 2.1: Systems and Center of Mass

**LO 2.1.A** – Describe properties and interactions of a system.  
**EK 2.1.A.1** – System properties determined by interactions between objects inside.  
**EK 2.1.A.2** – If internal structure unimportant, system treated as single object.  
**EK 2.1.A.3** – Systems may allow interactions with environment (energy/mass transfer).  
**EK 2.1.A.4** – Individual objects may behave differently from system as a whole.  
**EK 2.1.A.5** – Internal structure affects analysis.  
**EK 2.1.A.6** – Changing external variables may change system’s substructure.  
**LO 2.1.B** – Describe location of center of mass.  
**EK 2.1.B.1** – Symmetrical mass distributions: CM on lines of symmetry.  
**EK 2.1.B.2** – \(\overline{x}_{cm} = \frac{\sum m_i \overline{x}_i}{\sum m_i}\)  
**EK 2.1.B.3** – System can be modeled as single object at CM.  
**Boundary:** CM calculation for ≤5 particles in 2‑D or highly symmetrical.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.B, 2.B, 2.C, 3.B

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
Three masses (2 kg, 3 kg, 5 kg) are placed at positions x = 0, 2 m, and 5 m on a line. Where is the center of mass?  
(A) 2.5 m (B) 2.9 m (C) 3.1 m (D) 3.5 m → **Answer: B** ( (2*0 + 3*2 + 5*5)/(2+3+5)= (0+6+25)/10=31/10=3.1 m)

**Sample Word Problem:**  
Two children sit on a seesaw. Child A (mass 30 kg) sits 1.5 m from the pivot. Child B (mass 25 kg) sits on the other side. How far from the pivot must Child B sit for the seesaw to balance? (Ignore mass of seesaw.)  
*(Answer: 30*1.5 = 25*d → d = 1.8 m)*

#### Topic 2.2: Forces and Free‑Body Diagrams

**LO 2.2.A** – Describe force as interaction.  
**EK 2.2.A.1** – Forces are vectors. Each due to interaction with another object. An object cannot exert net force on itself.  
**EK 2.2.A.2** – Contact forces: macroscopic effects of interatomic electric forces.  
**LO 2.2.B** – Describe forces using free‑body diagram.  
**EK 2.2.B.1** – FBDs visualize forces, help write equations.  
**EK 2.2.B.2** – FBD shows forces exerted **on** the object by environment.  
**EK 2.2.B.3** – Vectors from dot (center of mass).  
**EK 2.2.B.4** – Choose coordinate axes with one parallel to acceleration.  
**Boundary:** FBDs show forces, not components; draw arrows side‑by‑side if same direction, not overlapping.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.A, 2.B, 2.C, 3.C

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
A book rests on a table. Which forces act on the book?  
(A) Weight only (B) Weight and normal force (C) Weight, normal, and friction (D) Normal force only → **Answer: B**

**Sample Free‑Response / Word Problem:**  
A box of mass 10 kg is pulled by a rope at an angle of 30° above horizontal with a force of 40 N. The box moves at constant speed across a rough horizontal floor.  
a) Draw a free‑body diagram showing all forces.  
b) Write Newton’s second law equations for x and y directions.  
c) Calculate the normal force.  
d) Calculate the kinetic friction force.  
*(Answers: c) N = mg - F sin30° = 100 - 20 = 80 N; d) f_k = F cos30° ≈ 34.6 N)*

#### Topic 2.3: Newton’s Third Law

**LO 2.3.A** – Describe interaction using Newton’s 3rd and paired forces.  
**EK 2.3.A.1** – \(\vec{F}_{\text{A on B}} = -\vec{F}_{\text{B on A}}\)  
**EK 2.3.A.2** – Internal forces do not affect motion of system’s CM.  
**EK 2.3.A.3** – Tension: macroscopic net result.  
- Ideal string: negligible mass, does not stretch → tension same at all points.  
- Non‑negligible mass: tension may vary.  
- Ideal pulley: negligible mass, negligible friction at axle.  
**Boundary:** Qualitative description of tension in massive string only (e.g., hanging chain – greater tension toward top).  
**Boundary:** Field forces only gravitational in this course.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.A, 2.D, 3.B, 3.C

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
A horse pulls a cart. The force the horse exerts on the cart is equal in magnitude to the force the cart exerts on the horse. Why does the system accelerate?  
(A) The forces are not a Newton’s third law pair. (B) The forces act on different objects. (C) The cart is heavier. (D) The horse is stronger. → **Answer: B**

**Sample Word Problem:**  
A 5 kg object and a 10 kg object are in contact on a frictionless horizontal surface. A force of 30 N is applied to the 5 kg object.  
a) Find the acceleration of the system.  
b) Find the force that the 5 kg object exerts on the 10 kg object.  
c) Find the force that the 10 kg object exerts on the 5 kg object.  
*(Answers: a) 30/15=2 m/s²; b) 10*2=20 N; c) 20 N (Newton’s 3rd))*

---

#### Topic 2.4: Newton’s First Law

**LO 2.4.A** – Describe conditions for constant velocity.  
**EK 2.4.A.1** – Net force = vector sum of all forces on system.  
**EK 2.4.A.2** – Translational equilibrium: \(\sum \vec{F} = 0\).  
**EK 2.4.A.3** – Newton’s 1st: if net force = 0, velocity constant.  
**EK 2.4.A.4** – Forces may be balanced in one dimension, unbalanced in another.  
**EK 2.4.A.5** – Inertial reference frame: Newton’s 1st holds.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.C, 2.A, 3.B, 3.C

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
A puck slides on ice at constant velocity. Which is true?  
(A) No forces act on the puck. (B) The net force is zero. (C) Gravity is zero. (D) Friction is greater than zero. → **Answer: B**

**Sample Word Problem:**  
A box is at rest on a horizontal surface. A horizontal force of 10 N is applied, but the box does not move.  
a) What is the net force on the box?  
b) What is the static friction force?  
c) If the applied force is increased to 15 N and the box just starts to move, what is the maximum static friction?  
*(Answers: a) 0 N; b) 10 N; c) 15 N)*

---

#### Topic 2.5: Newton’s Second Law

**LO 2.5.A** – Describe conditions for changing velocity.  
**EK 2.5.A.1** – Unbalanced forces → net force ≠ 0.  
**EK 2.5.A.2** – \(\vec{a}_{\text{sys}} = \frac{\sum \vec{F}}{m_{\text{sys}}} = \frac{\vec{F}_{\text{net}}}{m_{\text{sys}}}\)  
**EK 2.5.A.3** – Velocity of CM changes only if nonzero net external force.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.A, 2.A, 2.D, 3.B

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
A 2 kg object experiences a net force of 8 N. What is its acceleration?  
(A) 2 m/s² (B) 4 m/s² (C) 8 m/s² (D) 16 m/s² → **Answer: B**

**Sample Word Problem (Derivation):**  
A block of mass m slides down a frictionless incline of angle θ.  
a) Draw a free‑body diagram.  
b) Derive an expression for the acceleration of the block.  
c) If m = 5 kg and θ = 30°, calculate the acceleration. (Use g=10 m/s²)  
*(Answers: b) a = g sinθ; c) 5 m/s²)*

**Extended Problem (Two‑body system):**  
Two masses m1 = 3 kg and m2 = 2 kg are connected by a light string over a frictionless pulley. m1 hangs vertically, m2 is on a frictionless horizontal table.  
a) Draw FBDs for each mass.  
b) Derive an expression for the acceleration.  
c) Calculate the acceleration and the tension.  
*(Answers: b) a = m1g/(m1+m2); c) a = 6 m/s², T = 12 N (using g=10))*

#### Topic 2.6: Gravitational Force

**LO 2.6.A** – Describe gravitational interaction.  
**EK 2.6.A.1** – \(|\vec{F}_g| = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}\) (attractive, along line connecting CMs, acts at CM).  
**EK 2.6.A.2** – Field model: \(|\vec{g}| = \frac{|\vec{F}_g|}{m} = G\frac{M}{r^2}\). If gravity only force, acceleration numerically equals field strength.  
**EK 2.6.A.3** – Weight = \(F_g = mg\).  
**LO 2.6.B** – Situations where gravitational force constant.  
**EK 2.6.B.1** – If change in position negligibly changes force, treat constant.  
**EK 2.6.B.2** – Near Earth’s surface, \(g = 10\ \text{N/kg}\).  
**LO 2.6.C** – Conditions where apparent weight differs from gravitational force.  
**EK 2.6.C.1** – Apparent weight = magnitude of normal force.  
**EK 2.6.C.2** – If accelerating, apparent weight ≠ gravitational force.  
**EK 2.6.C.3** – Weightless when no forces or only gravity.  
**EK 2.6.C.4** – Equivalence principle: cannot distinguish between apparent weight and gravity in noninertial frame.  
**LO 2.6.D** – Inertial and gravitational mass.  
**EK 2.6.D.1** – Inertial mass = resistance to change in motion.  
**EK 2.6.D.2** – Gravitational mass = force of attraction.  
**EK 2.6.D.3** – Experimentally equivalent.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.A, 2.A, 2.D, 3.C

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
An astronaut in an orbiting space station feels weightless because:  
(A) There is no gravity in orbit. (B) The gravitational force is zero. (C) The astronaut and station are in free fall. (D) The station’s speed cancels gravity. → **Answer: C**

**Sample Word Problem:**  
A person of mass 70 kg stands on a scale in an elevator.  
a) What does the scale read when the elevator is at rest?  
b) What does it read when the elevator accelerates upward at 2 m/s²?  
c) What does it read when the elevator accelerates downward at 3 m/s²?  
*(Use g=10 m/s²; Answers: a) 700 N; b) 70*(10+2)=840 N; c) 70*(10-3)=490 N)*

**Derivation Problem:**  
Derive an expression for the escape speed from the surface of a planet of mass M and radius R. Start from conservation of energy.

#### Topic 2.7: Kinetic and Static Friction

**LO 2.7.A** – Describe kinetic friction.  
**EK 2.7.A.1** – Kinetic friction when surfaces move relative.  
- Direction opposite motion of each surface relative to the other.  
- Does **not** depend on contact area.  
**EK 2.7.A.2** – \(|\vec{F}_{f,k}| = \mu_k |\vec{F}_n|\) (coefficient depends on materials). Normal force = perpendicular component, directed away from surface.  
**LO 2.7.B** – Describe static friction.  
**EK 2.7.B.1** – Static friction when no relative motion.  
**EK 2.7.B.2** – \(|\vec{F}_{f,s}| \leq \mu_s |\vec{F}_n|\) ; maximum \(F_{f,s,\text{max}} = \mu_s F_n\).  
**EK 2.7.B.3** – Typically \(\mu_s > \mu_k\) for same pair.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.C, 2.B, 2.C, 3.B

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
A block of mass 5 kg is on a horizontal surface with μs = 0.4 and μk = 0.3. A horizontal force of 15 N is applied. Will the block move? (g=10 m/s²)  
(A) Yes, because 15 > μs*mg (20 N) (B) No, because 15 < 20 (C) Yes, because 15 > μk*mg (15 N) (D) No, because friction increases to match. → **Answer: B** (max static = 20 N, so 15 N not enough)

**Sample Word Problem:**  
A box of mass 12 kg is on a ramp inclined at 25° above horizontal. The coefficients of friction are μs=0.5, μk=0.3.  
a) Does the box slide on its own? (Check if mg sinθ > μs mg cosθ)  
b) If it slides, find the acceleration.  
*(Answers: a) mg sin25°≈50.7 N, μs mg cos25°≈54.4 N → not slide; b) not sliding)*

**Extended Problem:**  
A 10 kg box is pulled by a rope at 20° above horizontal with force F. μk=0.2.  
a) Derive an expression for the acceleration as a function of F.  
b) Find F needed to accelerate at 1 m/s². (g=10)  
*(Answer: b) a=(F cosθ - μk(mg - F sinθ))/m → solve: F≈33.5 N)*

#### Topic 2.8: Spring Forces

**LO 2.8.A** – Describe force from ideal spring.  
**EK 2.8.A.1** – Ideal spring: negligible mass, force proportional to change in length from relaxed length.  
**EK 2.8.A.2** – Hooke’s law: \(\vec{F}_s = -k \Delta \vec{x}\)  
**EK 2.8.A.3** – Force always toward equilibrium.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.B, 2.A, 2.C, 3.A, 3.B

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
A spring has spring constant 200 N/m. How much force is needed to stretch it by 0.05 m?  
(A) 2 N (B) 10 N (C) 20 N (D) 1000 N → **Answer: B**

**Sample Word Problem:**  
A spring has natural length 15 cm. When a 0.5 kg mass is hung from it, the length becomes 22 cm.  
a) What is the spring constant? (g=10)  
b) How much work is done to stretch it from 15 cm to 22 cm?  
c) If the mass is pulled down an additional 3 cm and released, what is the net force on the mass at that instant?  
*(Answers: a) 0.5*10=5 N, Δx=0.07 m → k=5/0.07≈71.4 N/m; b) ½*71.4*(0.07)²≈0.175 J; c) F=71.4*0.03=2.14 N upward)*

#### Topic 2.9: Circular Motion

**LO 2.9.A** – Describe motion in a circular path.  
**EK 2.9.A.1** – Centripetal acceleration \(a_c = v^2 / r\) (toward center).  
**EK 2.9.A.2** – Centripetal acceleration can come from single force, multiple forces, or components.  
- At top of vertical loop, minimum speed \(v = \sqrt{gr}\) (gravity only).  
- Banked curve (no friction): components of normal force contribute.  
- Conical pendulum: component of tension.  
**EK 2.9.A.3** – Tangential acceleration = rate of change of speed, tangent to circle.  
**EK 2.9.A.4** – Net acceleration = vector sum of centripetal and tangential.  
**EK 2.9.A.5** – Period T, frequency f: \(T = 1/f\). For constant speed: \(T = 2\pi r / v\).  
**LO 2.9.B** – Circular orbits using Kepler’s third law.  
**EK 2.9.B.1** – For satellite in circular orbit: \(T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2}{GM} R^3\) (centripetal = gravity).  
**Boundary:** Banked curves – quantitative only when no friction required; qualitative if friction needed.  
**Boundary:** Kepler’s 1st & 2nd laws not required.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.B, 2.A, 2.D, 3.A, 3.C

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
A car goes around a curve of radius 50 m at 20 m/s. What is the centripetal acceleration?  
(A) 0.4 m/s² (B) 2.5 m/s² (C) 8 m/s² (D) 10 m/s² → **Answer: C** (20²/50=8)

**Sample Word Problem:**  
A 0.5 kg ball is attached to a 0.8 m string and swung in a vertical circle. At the top of the circle, the speed is 4 m/s.  
a) What is the tension at the top? (g=10)  
b) What is the minimum speed at the top to keep the string taut?  
c) If the speed at the bottom is 8 m/s, find the tension at the bottom.  
*(Answers: a) T = mv²/r - mg = 0.5*16/0.8 - 5 = 10-5=5 N; b) v_min = √(gr)=√8≈2.83 m/s; c) T = mv²/r + mg = 0.5*64/0.8 +5 = 40+5=45 N)*

**Derivation Problem:**  
Derive the expression for the angle of a banked curve (no friction) that allows a car to travel at speed v without needing friction. Express in terms of v, r, g.

### UNIT 3 – WORK, ENERGY, AND POWER (18–23%, ~22–27 periods)

**Essential Questions:** Saving money charging phone? Energy conserved why running out? Does pushing always change energy? Why ramp easier than stairs?

**Developing Understanding:** Conservation, work as energy transfer.

**Building Science Practices:** 1.A, 1.C, 2.A, 3.C – graphs, energy bar charts, justifications.

**Preparing for Final Assessment:** Mathematical Routines (MR) questions – derive, calculate, make & justify claim.

#### Topic 3.1: Translational Kinetic Energy

**LO 3.1.A** – Describe translational kinetic energy.  
**EK 3.1.A.1** – \(K = \frac12 m v^2\)  
**EK 3.1.A.2** – Scalar.  
**EK 3.1.A.3** – Different observers may measure different K depending on frame.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.C, 2.B, 3.B, 3.C

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**

*(Answers: a) 60,000 J; b) 240,000 J; c) 180,000 J)*

#### Topic 3.2: Work

**LO 3.2.A** – Describe work done by force(s).  
**EK 3.2.A.1** – Work = energy transferred by force over distance.  
- Conservative force: path‑independent, depends only on initial/final configuration; zero if return to initial. Potential energy associated only with conservative forces.  
- Nonconservative force: path‑dependent (friction, air resistance).  
**EK 3.2.A.2** – Work scalar, can be positive, negative, zero.  
**EK 3.2.A.3** – \(W = F_{\parallel} d = F d \cos\theta\). Component perpendicular changes direction but not kinetic energy.  
**EK 3.2.A.4** – Work‑energy theorem: \(\Delta K = \sum W_i = \sum F_{\parallel,i} d_i\).  
- If force application point moves same distance as CM, system modeled as object, only kinetic energy changes.  
- Energy dissipated by friction: \(\Delta E_{\text{mch}} = F_f d \cos\theta\).  
**EK 3.2.A.5** – Work = area under \(F_{\parallel}\) vs. displacement graph.  
**Boundary:** Only mechanical energy transfer; thermal/sound dissipation noted but not analyzed in depth.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.B, 2.B, 2.D, 3.A, 3.B

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
A force of 10 N is applied horizontally to push a box 5 m across a floor. The force of kinetic friction is 3 N. The net work done on the box is:  
(A) 15 J (B) 35 J (C) 50 J (D) 65 J → **Answer: B** ( (10-3)*5=35 J)

**Sample Word Problem:**  
A 5 kg box is pulled 8 m along a horizontal surface by a rope at 30° above horizontal with a force of 30 N. The coefficient of kinetic friction is 0.2.  
a) Calculate the work done by the pulling force.  
b) Calculate the work done by friction.  
c) Calculate the net work and the final speed if the box started from rest. (g=10)  
*(Answers: a) 30*cos30°*8≈207.8 J; b) μk*(mg - F sin30°)*8 = 0.2*(50-15)*8=0.2*35*8=56 J, negative; c) net work=207.8-56=151.8 J, v=√(2*151.8/5)≈7.8 m/s)*

**Graphical Problem:**  
The force parallel to displacement varies as follows: from 0 to 2 m, F=5 N; from 2 to 5 m, F=10 N; from 5 to 6 m, F=0 N. Calculate the total work done.

#### Topic 3.3: Potential Energy

**LO 3.3.A** – Describe potential energy of system.  
**EK 3.3.A.1** – System of two+ objects interacting only via conservative forces has PE.  
**EK 3.3.A.2** – Scalar, depends on positions.  
**EK 3.3.A.3** – Zero point chosen by observer.  
**EK 3.3.A.4** – Elastic PE (ideal spring): \(U_s = \frac12 k (\Delta x)^2\)  
- Gravitational PE (general): \(U_g = -G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r}\)  
- Near surface: \(\Delta U_g = mg \Delta y\) (g constant).  
**EK 3.3.A.5** – Total PE = sum of PE of each pair.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.C, 2.C, 2.D, 3.B

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
A spring with k=200 N/m is compressed by 0.1 m. The potential energy stored is:  
(A) 1 J (B) 2 J (C) 10 J (D) 20 J → **Answer: A** (½*200*0.01=1)

**Sample Word Problem:**  
A 0.5 kg mass is lifted from the ground to a height of 2 m. (g=10)  
a) What is the change in gravitational potential energy?  
b) If the same mass is then lowered to a height of 0.5 m, what is the new change relative to ground?  
c) If we set the zero of PE at the 1 m height, what is the PE at 2 m and at ground?  
*(Answers: a) 10 J; b) ΔU = mg(0.5-2)= -7.5 J; c) at 2 m: mg(1)=5 J, at ground: mg(-1)= -5 J)*

**Derivation Problem:**  
Derive the expression for the gravitational potential energy of a system of two point masses starting from the work done by gravity.

#### Topic 3.4: Conservation of Energy

**LO 3.4.A** – Describe energies present in a system.  
**EK 3.4.A.1** – Single‑object system: only kinetic energy.  
**EK 3.4.A.2** – System with conservative forces or reversible shape change: KE + PE.  
**LO 3.4.B** – Describe behavior using conservation of mechanical energy.  
**EK 3.4.B.1** – Mechanical energy = KE + PE.  
**EK 3.4.B.2** – Change in one energy type balanced by change in another or transfer.  
**EK 3.4.B.3** – Choose system so total energy constant.  
**EK 3.4.B.4** – Change in total energy = energy transferred in/out.  
**LO 3.4.C** – How system selection determines whether energy changes.  
**EK 3.4.C.1** – Energy conserved in all interactions.  
**EK 3.4.C.2** – If work done on system = 0 and no nonconservative interactions, total mechanical energy constant.  
**EK 3.4.C.3** – If work done on system ≠ 0, energy transferred.  
**Boundary:** Know mechanical energy can be dissipated as thermal or sound.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.A, 2.A, 2.C, 3.C

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
A 2 kg block slides down a frictionless incline from a height of 5 m. Its speed at the bottom is (g=10):  
(A) 5 m/s (B) 7.1 m/s (C) 10 m/s (D) 14.1 m/s → **Answer: C** (mgh=½mv² → v=√(2gh)=√100=10)

**Sample Word Problem:**  
A roller coaster car (mass 500 kg) starts from rest at a height of 30 m. There is no friction except for a 10 m horizontal section with μk=0.2 at the bottom.  
a) Find the speed at the bottom of the first drop.  
b) How far does it travel along the rough section before stopping?  
*(Answers: a) v=√(2*10*30)=24.5 m/s; b) work done by friction = μk mg d = 0.2*500*10*d = 1000d, set equal to initial KE=½*500*600=150,000 J → d=150 m)*

**Energy Bar Chart Problem:**  
A block attached to a spring is pulled to x = +A and released. Draw energy bar charts (U_spring and K) at x = +A, x = 0, and x = -A. Label relative heights.

#### Topic 3.5: Power

**LO 3.5.A** – Describe energy transfer in terms of power.  
**EK 3.5.A.1** – Power = rate of energy change (transfer or conversion).  
**EK 3.5.A.2** – \(P_{\text{avg}} = \Delta E / \Delta t\)  
**EK 3.5.A.3** – \(P_{\text{avg}} = W / \Delta t\)  
**EK 3.5.A.4** – Instantaneous power for constant force: \(P_{\text{inst}} = F_{\parallel} v = F v \cos\theta\)

**Suggested Skills:** 1.B, 2.A, 2.C, 3.A, 3.C

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
A motor lifts a 100 kg crate at constant speed of 2 m/s. What power is required? (g=10)

**Sample Word Problem:**

b) If the force is constant, how much work is done in 10 seconds?  
c) If the car has mass 1200 kg, what is its acceleration at that speed (ignoring other forces)?  
*(Answers: a) F = P/v = 50000/25 = 2000 N; b) W = P*t = 50000*10 = 500,000 J; c) a = F/m = 2000/1200 ≈ 1.67 m/s²)*

### UNIT 4 – LINEAR MOMENTUM (10–15%, ~10–15 periods)

**Essential Questions:** Physics momentum vs everyday usage? Jumping in falling elevator? Water balloon on pavement vs caught? Crumple zones?

**Developing Understanding:** Force, time, impulse, momentum; conservation; connections to Newton’s 3rd and kinetic energy.

**Building Science Practices:** 1.B, 2.D, 3.A – data collection, experimental design, linearization.

**Preparing for Final Assessment:** Experimental Design and Analysis (LAB) questions – design procedure, collect/linearize data, analyze.

---

#### Topic 4.1: Linear Momentum

**LO 4.1.A** – Describe linear momentum.  
**EK 4.1.A.1** – \(\vec{p} = m\vec{v}\)  
**EK 4.1.A.2** – Vector, same direction as velocity.  
**EK 4.1.A.3** – Momentum used for collisions and explosions.  
- Collision: interaction where internal forces >> net external.  
- Only initial/final states analyzed – object model.  
- Explosion: internal forces move objects apart.  
**Boundary:** “Momentum” means linear momentum unless stated.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.C, 2.B, 2.C, 3.B

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
A 0.2 kg baseball moves at 30 m/s. Its momentum is:  
(A) 6 kg·m/s (B) 60 kg·m/s (C) 0.006 kg·m/s (D) 150 kg·m/s → **Answer: A**

**Sample Word Problem:**  
A 1500 kg car traveling east at 20 m/s collides with a 2000 kg truck traveling west at 10 m/s.  
a) Find the total momentum of the system before collision (magnitude and direction).  
b) If they stick together, find the velocity after collision.  
*(Answers: a) p_car = 30,000 east, p_truck = 20,000 west → net = 10,000 east; b) 10,000 = (1500+2000)v → v ≈ 2.86 m/s east)*

#### Topic 4.2: Change in Momentum and Impulse

**LO 4.2.A** – Describe impulse delivered.  
**EK 4.2.A.1** – \(\vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \Delta \vec{p} / \Delta t\)  
**EK 4.2.A.2** – \(\vec{J} = \vec{F}_{\text{avg}} \Delta t\)  
**EK 4.2.A.3** – Impulse vector, same direction as net force.  
**EK 4.2.A.4** – Impulse = area under F‑t curve.  
**EK 4.2.A.5** – Net force = slope of p‑t graph.  
**LO 4.2.B** – Relate impulse to change in momentum.  
**EK 4.2.B.1** – \(\Delta \vec{p} = \vec{p} - \vec{p}_0\)  
**EK 4.2.B.2** – Impulse‑momentum theorem: \(\vec{J} = \Delta \vec{p}\)  
**EK 4.2.B.3** – Newton’s 2nd as result for constant mass: \(\vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \Delta \vec{p}/\Delta t = m\vec{a}\)  
**Boundary:** No quantitative analysis of systems with changing mass.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.B, 2.A, 2.D, 3.A, 3.C

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
A 0.1 kg ball hits a wall at 10 m/s and rebounds at 8 m/s. The magnitude of the impulse on the ball is:  
(A) 0.2 N·s (B) 0.8 N·s (C) 1.0 N·s (D) 1.8 N·s → **Answer: D** (Δp = 0.1*(8-(-10)) = 0.1*18 = 1.8)

**Sample Word Problem:**  
A force‑time graph shows a triangular shape: from t=0 to 2 s, force increases linearly from 0 to 10 N; from t=2 to 4 s, force decreases linearly back to 0.  
a) Calculate the impulse delivered.  
b) If this force acts on a 2 kg object initially at rest, what is its final speed?  
*(Answers: a) area = ½*4*10 = 20 N·s; b) v = 20/2 = 10 m/s)*

**Design Problem:**  
Design an experiment to measure the impulse delivered by a spring‑loaded plunger to a cart on a frictionless track. Describe how you would measure force and time, or how you would use motion sensors to determine change in momentum.

#### Topic 4.3: Conservation of Linear Momentum

**LO 4.3.A** – Describe behavior using conservation.  
**EK 4.3.A.1** – Collection of objects → system with CM velocity.  
- \(\vec{v}_{\text{cm}} = \frac{\sum \vec{p}_i}{\sum m_i} = \frac{\sum m_i \vec{v}_i}{\sum m_i}\)  
- \(\vec{v}_{\text{cm}}\) constant if no net external force.  
**EK 4.3.A.2** – Total momentum = sum of momenta of parts.  
**EK 4.3.A.3** – No net external force → change in one object balanced by opposite change elsewhere. Any change in system momentum due to transfer with surroundings.  
- Impulses from Newton’s 3rd: equal and opposite.  
- Choose system so total momentum constant.  
- If total momentum changes, change = impulse on system: \(\vec{J} = \Delta \vec{p}\).  
**EK 4.3.A.4** – Conservation of momentum determines velocities immediately before/after collisions or explosions.  
**Boundary:** Course: quantitative & qualitative in 1‑D; semiquantitative in 2‑D (set up equations but not solve simultaneous; reason about changes).  
**LO 4.3.B** – How system selection determines whether momentum changes.  
**EK 4.3.B.1** – Momentum conserved in all interactions.  
**EK 4.3.B.2** – If net external force = 0, total momentum constant.  
**EK 4.3.B.3** – If net external force ≠ 0, momentum transferred.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.A, 2.A, 2.D, 3.C

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
Two ice skaters push off from each other. One has mass 50 kg and moves at 2 m/s. The other has mass 70 kg. What is her speed?  
(A) 1.43 m/s (B) 2.0 m/s (C) 2.8 m/s (D) 0 m/s → **Answer: A** (50*2 = 70*v → v=100/70≈1.43)

**Sample Word Problem (2‑D momentum – qualitative):**  
A 3 kg object moving east at 4 m/s collides with a 2 kg object moving north at 3 m/s. They stick together.  
a) Set up the conservation of momentum equations for x and y components.  
b) Calculate the final speed and direction.  
c) Is the collision elastic? Justify.  
*(Answers: b) px:12 = (5)v_fx → v_fx=2.4; py:6 = (5)v_fy → v_fy=1.2; v_f=√(2.4²+1.2²)=2.68 m/s; θ=arctan(1.2/2.4)=26.6°; c) KE initial = ½*3*16+½*2*9=24+9=33 J; KE final = ½*5*(2.68²)=17.96 J, so inelastic)*

**Qualitative Reasoning Problem:**  
A rifle fires a bullet. Explain, using conservation of momentum and Newton’s third law, why the rifle recoils backward even though the bullet moves forward.

#### Topic 4.4: Elastic and Inelastic Collisions

**LO 4.4.A** – Describe whether interaction is elastic or inelastic.  
**EK 4.4.A.1** – Elastic: total KE initial = total KE final.  
**EK 4.4.A.2** – In elastic, individual KE may change.  
**EK 4.4.A.3** – Inelastic: total KE decreases.  
**EK 4.4.A.4** – Some initial KE transformed by nonconservative forces.  
**EK 4.4.A.5** – Perfectly inelastic: objects stick together, same final velocity.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.B, 2.A, 2.C, 3.A, 3.B

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**

*(Answers: a) 1*3 = 1*(-1) + 2*v → 3 = -1+2v → v=2 m/s; b) KE initial = 4.5 J; KE final = ½*1*1 + ½*2*4 = 0.5+4=4.5 J → elastic)*

### UNIT 5 – TORQUE AND ROTATIONAL DYNAMICS (10–15%, ~15–20 periods)

**Essential Questions:** Door handle placement? Long wrenches? Mobiles vs Grand Canyon Skywalk? Tightrope walker’s long pole?

**Developing Understanding:** Rotational analogs of force, motion, dynamics.

**Building Science Practices:** 2.A, 2.C, 2.D – derivations, comparisons, functional dependence.

#### Topic 5.1: Rotational Kinematics

**LO 5.1.A** – Describe rotation using angular displacement, velocity, acceleration.  
**EK 5.1.A.1** – \(\Delta \theta = \theta - \theta_0\) (radians).  
- Rigid system: shape fixed, but different points move differently. Cannot model as object if rotation matters.  
- Direction (CW/CCW) designated positive/negative.  
- If rotation can be described by CM motion, treat as object (e.g., Earth’s revolution).  
**EK 5.1.A.2** – \(\omega_{\text{avg}} = \Delta \theta / \Delta t\)  
**EK 5.1.A.3** – \(\alpha_{\text{avg}} = \Delta \omega / \Delta t\)  
**EK 5.1.A.4** – Constant α:  
\(\omega = \omega_0 + \alpha t\)  
\(\theta = \theta_0 + \omega_0 t + \frac12 \alpha t^2\)  
\(\omega^2 = \omega_0^2 + 2\alpha(\theta - \theta_0)\)  
- Graphs of θ, ω, α vs. t.  
**Boundary:** Direction descriptions limited to CW/CCW.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.B, 2.A, 2.D, 3.A, 3.C

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**

**Sample Word Problem:**

a) Find the angular acceleration.  
b) How many revolutions does it make in that time?  
c) If the radius is 0.2 m, what is the tangential acceleration of a point on the rim?  
*(Answers: a) α = 30 rad/s²; b) θ = ½*30*100=1500 rad → 1500/(2π)≈238.7 rev; c) a_t = rα = 6 m/s²)*

#### Topic 5.2: Connecting Linear and Rotational Motion

**EK 5.2.A.2** – \(v = r \omega\), \(a_T = r \alpha\)  
**EK 5.2.A.3** – All points have same ω and α.

#### Topic 5.3: Torque

**LO 5.3.A** – Identify torques on a rigid system.  
**EK 5.3.A.1** – Torque from force component perpendicular to position vector from axis.  
**EK 5.3.A.2** – Lever arm = perpendicular distance from axis to line of action.  
**LO 5.3.B** – Describe torques using force diagrams.  
**EK 5.3.B.1** – Force diagrams similar to FBD but also show location of force application relative to axis.

**Sample Word Problem:**  
A 30 N force is applied to a door at an angle of 60° to the door surface (so 30° from perpendicular?). Clarify: Torque = rF sinθ, where θ is angle between r and F. #### Topic 5.4: Rotational Inertia

**LO 5.4.A** – Describe rotational inertia.  
**EK 5.4.A.1** – Rotational inertia measures resistance to change in rotation; depends on mass and distribution.  
**EK 5.4.A.2** – Point mass: \(I = m r^2\)  
**EK 5.4.A.3** – Collection: \(I_{\text{tot}} = \sum m_i r_i^2\)  
**LO 5.4.B** – Describe rotational inertia about axis not through CM.  
**EK 5.4.B.1** – Minimum when axis passes through CM.  
**EK 5.4.B.2** – Parallel axis theorem: \(I' = I_{\text{cm}} + M d^2\)  
**Boundary:** Only calculate I for ≤5 objects in 2‑D. Extended objects’ I given on exam. Qualitative understanding: greater mass farther from axis → greater I (hoop vs disk).

**Suggested Skills:** 1.B, 2.B, 2.C, 3.A, 3.B

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
Two point masses, 2 kg and 3 kg, are placed at distances 0.5 m and 1 m from an axis. The total rotational inertia is:  
(A) 2.5 kg·m² (B) 3.5 kg·m² (C) 4.5 kg·m² (D) 5.5 kg·m² → **Answer: B** (2*(0.5)² + 3*(1)² = 0.5+3=3.5)

**Sample Word Problem:**  
A solid disk (I_cm = ½MR²) of mass 2 kg and radius 0.1 m is pivoted at a point 0.05 m from its center. Use the parallel axis theorem to find the moment of inertia about that pivot.  
*(Answer: I = ½*2*0.01 + 2*(0.05)² = 0.01 + 0.005 = 0.015 kg·m²)*

#### Topic 5.5: Rotational Equilibrium and Newton’s First Law (Rotational)

**LO 5.5.A** – Describe conditions for constant angular velocity.  
**EK 5.5.A.1** – Rotational equilibrium possible without translational equilibrium and vice versa.  
- Force diagrams / FBDs describe forces and torques.  
- Rotational equilibrium: \(\sum \tau_i = 0\)  
- Rotational Newton’s 1st: constant angular velocity if net torque = 0.  
- If torques unbalanced, angular velocity changes.  
**Boundary:** No simultaneous analysis of rotation in multiple planes.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.C, 2.A, 2.B, 3.B

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
A seesaw is balanced with a 30 kg child on one side at 1.5 m from the pivot and a 20 kg child on the other side. The distance of the 20 kg child from the pivot is:  
(A) 1.0 m (B) 1.5 m (C) 2.25 m (D) 3.0 m → **Answer: C** (30*1.5 = 20*d → d=45/20=2.25)

**Sample Word Problem:**  
A uniform beam of mass 5 kg and length 2 m is supported at both ends. A 10 kg mass is placed 0.5 m from the left end. Find the forces at each support. (Use g=10)  
*(Answer: let left support F1, right F2. Sum forces: F1+F2 = (5+10)*10=150 N. Sum torques about left: 10*10*0.5 + 5*10*1 = F2*2 → 50+50=2F2 → F2=50 N, then F1=100 N)*

#### Topic 5.6: Newton’s Second Law in Rotational Form

**LO 5.6.A** – Describe conditions for changing angular velocity.  
**EK 5.6.A.1** – Angular velocity changes if net torque ≠ 0.  
**EK 5.6.A.2** – \(\alpha_{\text{sys}} = \frac{\sum \tau}{I_{\text{sys}}} = \frac{\tau_{\text{net}}}{I_{\text{sys}}}\)  
**EK 5.6.A.3** – To fully describe rotating rigid system, may need both linear and rotational analyses.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.A, 2.A, 2.C, 3.C

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
A disk of moment of inertia 0.2 kg·m² experiences a net torque of 0.5 N·m. Its angular acceleration is:  
(A) 0.1 rad/s² (B) 2.5 rad/s² (C) 0.4 rad/s² (D) 1.0 rad/s² → **Answer: B** (α = τ/I = 0.5/0.2=2.5)

**Sample Word Problem (Combined linear + rotational):**  
A yo‑yo consists of a solid disk of mass 0.1 kg and radius 0.02 m (I=½MR²). The string unwinds from a central axle of radius 0.005 m. The yo‑yo falls from rest.  
a) Draw a force diagram (tension and weight).  
b) Write Newton’s second law for translation and for rotation.  
c) Derive an expression for the linear acceleration.  
d) Calculate the acceleration. (g=10)  
*(Answers: b) mg - T = ma; τ = T r = I α; with a = r α → T = I a/r². Then mg - (I a/r²) = ma → a = mg / (m + I/r²). I = ½(0.1)(0.02)² = 2e-5 kg·m², r=0.005 → I/r² = 2e-5/(2.5e-5)=0.8 kg. So a = (0.1*10)/(0.1+0.8)=1/0.9≈1.11 m/s²)*

**Essential Questions:** What keeps bicycle balanced? Planets move faster near sun? Satellites and projectiles common? Ice skater arms?

#### Topic 6.1: Rotational Kinetic Energy

**LO 6.1.A** – Describe rotational KE.  
**EK 6.1.A.1** – \(K_{\text{rot}} = \frac12 I \omega^2\)  
- Rotational inertia about fixed axis → rotational KE equivalent to total KE if purely rotational.

**EK 6.1.A.2** – A system can have rotational KE even if CM at rest (points moving).  
**EK 6.1.A.3** – Scalar.

#### Topic 6.2: Torque and Work

**LO 6.2.A** – Describe work done by torque(s).  
**EK 6.2.A.1** – Torque can transfer energy if applied over angular displacement.  
**EK 6.2.A.2** – \(W = \tau \Delta \theta\)  
**EK 6.2.A.3** – Work = area under τ‑θ graph.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.B, 2.A, 2.C, 2.D, 3.A

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**

#### Topic 6.3: Angular Momentum and Angular Impulse

**LO 6.3.A** – Describe angular momentum.  
**EK 6.3.A.1** – For rigid system: \(L = I \omega\)  
**EK 6.3.A.2** – For point object: \(L = r m v \sin\theta\) (depends on reference point; for straight‑line motion, depends on distance, mass, speed, angle).  
**LO 6.3.B** – Describe angular impulse delivered by torque.  
**EK 6.3.B.1** – Angular impulse = \(\tau \Delta t\)  
**EK 6.3.B.2** – Same direction as torque.  
**EK 6.3.B.3** – Angular impulse = area under τ‑t graph.  
**LO 6.3.C** – Relate change in angular momentum to angular impulse.  
**EK 6.3.C.1** – \(\Delta L = L - L_0\)  
**EK 6.3.C.2** – Rotational impulse‑momentum theorem: \(\Delta L = \tau \Delta t\)  
- For constant I: \(\tau_{\text{net}} = \Delta L/\Delta t = I \alpha\)  
**EK 6.3.C.3** – Net torque = slope of L‑t graph.  
**EK 6.3.C.4** – Angular impulse = area under τ‑t graph.  
**Boundary:** Only magnitude of angular momentum/impulse; direction beyond scope.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.B, 2.A, 2.D, 3.B

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
A disk of I=0.5 kg·m² rotates at 20 rad/s. Its angular momentum is:  
(A) 10 kg·m²/s (B) 40 kg·m²/s (C) 0.025 kg·m²/s (D) 0.5 kg·m²/s → **Answer: A**

**Sample Word Problem:**  
A 0.2 kg particle moves in a straight line at 15 m/s. Its distance from a pivot is 0.8 m, and the line of motion is perpendicular to the radius. What is its angular momentum about the pivot?  
*(Answer: L = r m v = 0.8*0.2*15 = 2.4 kg·m²/s)*

**Graph Problem:**  
Torque vs time graph: from 0 to 2 s, τ=2 N·m; from 2 to 5 s, τ=0; from 5 to 7 s, τ=-1 N·m. Find the angular impulse from t=0 to t=7 s.  
*(Answer: area = 2*2 + 0 + (-1)*2 = 4-2=2 N·m·s)*

#### Topic 6.4: Conservation of Angular Momentum

**LO 6.4.A** – Describe behavior using conservation of angular momentum.  
**EK 6.4.A.1** – Total angular momentum = sum of parts.  
**EK 6.4.A.2** – Change in system’s angular momentum only by interaction with surroundings.  
- Angular impulses are equal and opposite (Newton’s 3rd).  
- Choose system so total angular momentum constant.  
- Nonrigid system: shape change (mass closer/farther from axis) changes ω without changing L.  
- If total L changes, change = angular impulse.  
**LO 6.4.B** – How system selection determines change.  
**EK 6.4.B.1** – Angular momentum conserved in all interactions.  
**EK 6.4.B.2** – If net external torque = 0, total L constant.  
**EK 6.4.B.3** – If net external torque ≠ 0, L transferred.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.B, 2.D, 3.A, 3.B, 3.C

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
An ice skater spins with arms outstretched at 2 rev/s. She pulls her arms in, reducing her moment of inertia by half. Her new angular speed is:  
(A) 1 rev/s (B) 2 rev/s (C) 4 rev/s (D) 8 rev/s → **Answer: C** (L constant → Iω constant → ω doubles)

**Sample Word Problem:**  
A student sits on a rotating stool with arms outstretched, holding a 2 kg mass in each hand. The student + stool have I=3 kg·m² (excluding masses). Each mass is at 0.8 m from the axis. The system rotates at 1 rad/s.  
a) What is the total angular momentum?  
b) The student pulls the masses in to 0.2 m from the axis. What is the new angular speed?  
c) Explain why the speed increases.  
*(Answers: a) I_total initial = 3 + 2*(2*0.8²)=3+2.56=5.56, L=5.56*1=5.56; b) I_final = 3 + 2*(2*0.2²)=3+0.16=3.16, ω_f = L/I_f=5.56/3.16≈1.76 rad/s; c) No external torque, L conserved, I decreases so ω increases.)*

#### Topic 6.5: Rolling

**LO 6.5.A** – Describe KE of system with translational and rotational motion.  
**EK 6.5.A.1** – \(K_{\text{tot}} = K_{\text{trans}} + K_{\text{rot}}\)  
**LO 6.5.B** – Describe rolling without slipping.  
**EK 6.5.B.1** – \(\Delta x_{\text{cm}} = r \Delta \theta\), \(v_{\text{cm}} = r \omega\), \(a_{\text{cm}} = r \alpha\)  
**EK 6.5.B.2** – Ideal: no energy dissipated by friction.  
**LO 6.5.C** – Describe rolling while slipping.  
**EK 6.5.C.1** – When slipping, no direct relationship between CM motion and rotation.  
**EK 6.5.C.2** – Kinetic friction dissipates energy.  
**Boundary:** Rolling friction beyond scope. Quantitative analysis of rolling while slipping beyond scope; qualitative explanation only.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.A, 2.A, 2.C, 3.C

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**

(A) 10 J (B) 20 J (C) 30 J (D) 50 J → **Answer: B** (K_rot/K_trans = (½Iω²)/(½Mv²) = (½*(⅖MR²)*(v²/R²)) / (½Mv²) = ⅖. So K_rot = (2/7) K_total? Actually K_total = K_trans + K_rot = K_trans(1+⅖)= (7/5)K_trans → K_trans = (5/7)K_total=50, K_rot=20 J)

A hoop (I=MR²) and a solid disk (I=½MR²) of same mass and radius roll without slipping down an incline from the same height.  
a) Which has more total kinetic energy at the bottom? (Same – both convert same gravitational PE)  
b) Which has greater translational speed? Compare.  
*(Answer: For rolling object, mgh = ½mv² + ½Iω² = ½mv²(1 + I/(MR²)). For hoop: I/(MR²)=1 → v = √(gh); for disk: I/(MR²)=0.5 → v = √(4gh/3). Disk has higher speed.)*

#### Topic 6.6: Motion of Orbiting Satellites

**LO 6.6.A** – Describe motions of two objects interacting only via gravity.  
**EK 6.6.A.1** – If satellite mass negligible compared to central object, central object’s motion negligible.  
**EK 6.6.A.2** – Motions constrained by conservation laws.  
- Circular orbits: total mechanical energy, gravitational PE, satellite’s L and KE constant.  
- Elliptical orbits: total mechanical energy and satellite’s L constant; gravitational PE and KE change.  
- Gravitational PE defined zero at infinite distance: \(U_g = -G m_1 m_2 / r\)  
**EK 6.6.A.3** – Escape velocity: mechanical energy = 0.  
- \(v_{\text{esc}} = \sqrt{2GM/r}\) (derived from conservation of energy).  
**Boundary:** Kepler’s 1st & 2nd not required.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.C, 2.A, 2.C, 3.C

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
A satellite in circular orbit around Earth has speed v. If the orbital radius is doubled, the new speed is:  
(A) v/2 (B) v/√2 (C) v (D) √2 v → **Answer: B** (GMm/r² = mv²/r → v = √(GM/r) → v ∝ 1/√r)

**Sample Word Problem:**  
A satellite orbits Earth at a distance of 2R_e (R_e = 6.37×10⁶ m, M_e = 5.98×10²⁴ kg, G=6.67×10⁻¹¹).  
a) Find the orbital speed.  
b) Find the orbital period.  
c) If the satellite’s speed were increased by 20%, would it escape? (Escape velocity from that altitude = √(2GM/r))  
*(Answers: a) v = √(GM/(2R_e)) ≈ √( (6.67e-11*5.98e24)/(1.274e7) ) = √(3.99e14/1.274e7)=√(3.13e7)=5590 m/s; b) T = 2πr/v = 2π*1.274e7/5590 ≈ 14300 s ≈ 3.97 h; c) v_esc = √2 * v ≈ 7900 m/s, 1.2v = 6708 m/s < 7900, so no escape)*

**Derivation Problem:**

#### Topic 7.1: Defining Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)

**LO 7.1.A** – Describe SHM.  
**EK 7.1.A.1** – SHM is a special case of periodic motion.  
**EK 7.1.A.2** – Restoring force magnitude ∝ displacement from equilibrium.  
- Derived: \(m a_x = -k \Delta x\)  
- Restoring force opposite displacement.  
- Equilibrium position: net force = 0.  
- Pendulum with small angular displacement: restoring torque ∝ angular displacement → modeled as SHM.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.C, 2.B, 3.B, 3.C

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
Which of the following is a necessary condition for SHM?  
(A) Constant acceleration (B) Force proportional to displacement (C) Force constant (D) Large amplitude → **Answer: B**

**Sample Word Problem:**  
A 0.5 kg mass is attached to a spring with k=20 N/m. It is pulled 0.1 m from equilibrium and released.  
a) What is the magnitude of the restoring force at release?  
b) What is the initial acceleration?  
c) Is this SHM? Why?  
*(Answers: a) F = kx = 20*0.1=2 N; b) a = F/m = 4 m/s²; c) Yes, because force is proportional to displacement and opposite direction.)*

#### Topic 7.2: Frequency and Period of SHM

**LO 7.2.A** – Describe frequency and period.  
**EK 7.2.A.1** – \(T = 1/f\)  
- Spring‑mass: \(T_s = 2\pi \sqrt{m/k}\)  
- Simple pendulum (small angle): \(T_p = 2\pi \sqrt{\ell/g}\)

**Suggested Skills:** 1.B, 2.A, 2.D, 3.A, 3.C

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
A mass‑spring system oscillates with period 2 s. If the mass is doubled, the new period is:  
(A) 1 s (B) 2 s (C) 2√2 s (D) 4 s → **Answer: C** (T ∝ √m)

**Sample Word Problem:**  
A pendulum has length 1 m (g=10).  
a) Calculate its period.  
b) If the pendulum is taken to the Moon where g=1.6 m/s², what is the new period?  
c) How long must a pendulum be on Earth to have a period of 1 s?  
*(Answers: a) T = 2π√(1/10)=2π*0.316≈1.99 s; b) T = 2π√(1/1.6)=2π*0.791≈4.97 s; c) L = g(T/(2π))² = 10*(1/(2π))²≈0.253 m)*

**Experimental Design Problem:**

**LO 7.3.A** – Describe displacement, velocity, acceleration in SHM.

#### Topic 7.4: Energy of Simple Harmonic Oscillators

**LO 7.4.A** – Describe mechanical energy in SHM.  
**EK 7.4.A.1** – \(E_{\text{total}} = U + K\)  
**EK 7.4.A.2** – Energy conserved in SHM.  
**EK 7.4.A.3** – KE max when PE min; PE max when KE min.  
**EK 7.4.A.4** – Minimum KE = 0.  
**EK 7.4.A.5** – Changing amplitude changes max PE and total energy. For spring: \(E_{\text{total}} = \frac12 k A^2\).

**Suggested Skills:** 1.A, 2.B, 2.C, 3.B

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
A spring‑mass system has total energy 10 J. When the displacement is half the amplitude, the kinetic energy is:

(A) 600 kg/m³ (B) 1000 kg/m³ (C) 400 kg/m³ (D) 1667 kg/m³ → **Answer: A** (mg = ρ_water * (0.6V) g → ρ_wood = 0.6*1000=600)

**Sample Word Problem:**  
A 0.2 kg metal cube of volume 2.5×10⁻⁵ m³ is placed in water.  
a) What is the buoyant force?  
b) What is the apparent weight (scale reading) when submerged? (g=10)  
c) Will it float? Why?  
*(Answers: a) F_b = ρ_water * V * g = 1000*2.5e-5*10 = 0.25 N; b) weight = 2 N, apparent weight = 2 - 0.25 = 1.75 N; c) It sinks because weight > buoyant force.)*

**Derivation Problem:**  
Derive Archimedes’ principle: the buoyant force equals the weight of the displaced fluid, starting from the pressure difference on a submerged object.

#### Topic 8.4: Fluids and Conservation Laws

**LO 8.4.A** – Describe flow of incompressible fluid using mass conservation.  
**EK 8.4.A.1** – Pressure difference causes flow.  
- Mass flow rate: \(V/t = A v\)  
**EK 8.4.A.2** – Continuity equation: \(A_1 v_1 = A_2 v_2\)  
**LO 8.4.B** – Describe fluid flow from energy difference.  
**EK 8.4.B.1** – Difference in gravitational PE leads to differences in KE and pressure (conservation).  
**EK 8.4.B.2** – Bernoulli’s equation: \(P_1 + \rho g y_1 + \frac12 \rho v_1^2 = P_2 + \rho g y_2 + \frac12 \rho v_2^2\)  
**EK 8.4.B.3** – Torricelli’s theorem: \(v = \sqrt{2g \Delta y}\) (derived from energy).  
**Boundary:** All fluids ideal, pipes completely filled.

**Suggested Skills:** 1.B, 2.A, 2.C, 3.A, 3.B

**Sample Multiple‑Choice Question:**  
Water flows through a pipe that narrows from 0.04 m² to 0.01 m². If the speed in the wide part is 2 m/s, the speed in the narrow part is:  
(A) 0.5 m/s (B) 2 m/s (C) 8 m/s (D) 16 m/s → **Answer: C** (A1v1=A2v2 → 0.04*2=0.01*v2 → v2=8)

**Sample Word Problem (Bernoulli):**  
Water flows through a horizontal pipe that constricts. At point 1, the pressure is 200 kPa, area 0.02 m², speed 1 m/s. At point 2, area is 0.005 m². (ρ=1000)  
a) Find the speed at point 2.  
b) Find the pressure at point 2.  
c) Is the pressure higher or lower in the constriction? Explain.  
*(Answers: a) v2 = (A1/A2)*v1 = (0.02/0.005)*1 = 4 m/s; b) Bernoulli: P1 + ½ρv1² = P2 + ½ρv2² → 200,000 + 500 = P2 + 8000 → P2 = 192,500 Pa; c) lower, because increased kinetic energy comes from pressure drop)*

**Torricelli Problem:**  
A tank of water has a hole 2 m below the water surface. Find the exit speed of the water. (g=10)  
*(Answer: v = √(2*10*2) = √40 ≈ 6.32 m/s)*

**Experimental Design Problem:**  
Design an experiment to verify Torricelli’s theorem. You have a large container with a small hole near the bottom, a meter stick, a stopwatch, and a beaker. Describe your procedure and how you would analyze the data.

## 11. Scoring Guidelines (Summarized for Each FRQ Type)

### 11.1 Mathematical Routines (MR) – Example (Disk rolling down ramp)
- Force diagram: gravity down, normal perpendicular, friction up ramp – 2 pts.
- Torque from friction – 1 pt.
- Net force expression (mgsinθ − Ff) – 1 pt.
- Multi‑step derivation using N2 translation & rotation, relation a = αR – 3 pts (derivation, relation, correct a = (2/3)g sinθ).
- Comparison of times: t_disk > t_block – 1 pt; indicate smaller acceleration – 1 pt; justification – 1 pt.

### 11.2 Translation Between Representations (TBR) – Example (Spring‑block oscillator)
- Energy bar charts at x=0 (only K) and x=+x0 (only Us) with equal total – 3 pts.
- k = Fmax/x0 – 1 pt.
- Derivation of mass from period: start T=2π√(m/k), substitute, get m = (t0/π)² (Fmax/x0) – 3 pts.
- v‑t sketch: same period, symmetric, sine shape – 3 pts.
- Consistency check: at t=1.5t0, v max → slope zero → a=0 → spring force zero, but given FBD shows spring force – inconsistent – 2 pts.

### 11.3 Experimental Design (LAB) – Example (Density of liquid, Torricelli)
- Quantities: pressure and height – 1 pt.
- Reduce uncertainty: multiple measurements or heights – 1 pt.
- Graph P vs h (or h vs P) – 1 pt; slope = ρg → ρ = slope/g – 1 pt.
- Graph v² vs Δy – 1 pt; label axis with scale – 1 pt; plot points – 1 pt; best‑fit line – 1 pt.
- Calculate g from slope: slope/2 – 2 pts.

### 11.4 Qualitative/Quantitative Translation (QQT) – Example (Collision, M2 << M1)
- Claim vf ≈ v0 – 1 pt; justification using conservation of momentum / Newton’s 3rd – 2 pts (total 3).
- Derivation: M1v0 = (M1+M2)vf → vf = M1v0/(M1+M2) – 3 pts.
- Functional dependence: as M2→0, vf→v0, agrees – 2 pts.

## 12. Vocabulary and Definitions (Key Terms)

- **Model** – physical, mathematical, or conceptual representation of a system, ideas, events, or processes. Simplifies analysis.
- **Representation** – a depiction of a model (sketches, diagrams, graphs, equations, bar charts, FBDs, etc.).
- **Object** – a physical thing where internal structure and properties are ignored. No size, no internal motion. Treated as a point. Cannot be compressed, twisted, or rotated. Friction depends on surfaces but not contact area.
- **System** – a collection of objects analyzed together. Choice determines whether quantities are constant vs conserved.
- **Rigid System** – does not change shape, but different points may move differently. Cannot be modeled as an object when rotation matters.
- **Constant vs Conserved** – A quantity is **constant** if it does not change within the chosen system; it is **conserved** if the total amount in the universe does not change (may be transferred in/out of system).
